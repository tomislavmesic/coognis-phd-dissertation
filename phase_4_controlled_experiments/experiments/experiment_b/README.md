# Experiment B - Expert Interaction Evaluation Study

Experiment B evaluates whether matching an expert's communication style to a user's preferred communication style improves perceived interaction quality in simulated university support conversations.

## Study Objective

The objective is to test whether expert-user communication matching improves perceived ease, helpfulness, trust, willingness to contact the expert again, and perceived style match. The experiment extends the dissertation's investigation of psychological profiling from AI-generated support responses to expert interaction routing.

## Experimental Design

Participants first select a preferred answer style, which maps them to one of four communication personas: STJ, NTP, NFJ, or SFP. They then evaluate four simulated expert-user interactions in university support contexts. Each scenario compares a matched expert interaction with a mismatched expert interaction.

## Persona Logic

The study operationalizes four communication-style personas:

| Persona | Communication Style | Experimental Role |
| --- | --- | --- |
| STJ | Structured, procedural, step-by-step communication | Represents users who prefer ordered instructions and clear procedures. |
| NTP | Analytical, explanatory, reasoning-oriented communication | Represents users who prefer causal explanation and conceptual rationale. |
| NFJ | Supportive, contextual, reassurance-oriented communication | Represents users who prefer empathetic guidance with situational context. |
| SFP | Concise, practical, action-oriented communication | Represents users who prefer short, direct instructions. |

## Scenario Structure

Each scenario includes a university support context, a set of core facts held constant across conditions, matched interactions, and mismatched interactions. The four scenarios cover suspicious email handling, course schedule conflict, Wi-Fi access, and thesis topic change.

## Evaluation Workflow

For each interaction, participants evaluate ease, helpfulness, trust, likelihood of contacting the expert again, and perceived communication-style match. A binary contact-again item and closing control questions capture overall interaction preference and confidence in using an expert-routing support system.
